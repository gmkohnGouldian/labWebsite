People

 
Gregory M. Kohn, Ph.D: My research is centered on social niche construction. I seek to understand how birds experience and construct their social environments across development. To do this, I combine novel observational approaches to measure behavior in situ along with controlled perturbations of flocks in semi-naturalistic contexts. I am especially interested in how micro-level behavioral choices made on moment-by-moment timescales (Should I approach you? Should I preen with you?) both shape, and are shaped by, the macro-level social organization of the whole group. By taking a comparative approach, I hope to show how the interaction between social organization, behavioral choices, and development evolved across different avian lineages. I’m also interested in theoretical, historical, and philosophical approaches to understanding animal behavior and its development.

General note for those interested in grad school in my lab: Curiosity is the most essential value in my lab. If you can imagine yourself coming to the lab every day with a spirit of curiosity about the natural world and using that curiosity to overcome obstacles, persist despite being overwhelmed, read extensively about behavior, and spend a seemingly unnecessary and absurd amount of time observing birds (and enjoying it!), then my lab might be the right one for you.


Thomas Mateo: I am an ornithologist with a research focus on animal behavior but with a breadth of other interests, including evolution, ecology, and paleontology.  Previous work of mine includes research on the population dynamics of American and Saltwater Crocodiles, an investigation of dominance hierarchies in various marine invertebrates, and a test of the effects of acidic ocean conditions on the behavior of horseshoe crabs.  My current research topic is an investigation of peering behavior in the Gouldian Finch and how this mysterious behavior shapes juvenile development, social network formation, and implications for reproduction.  The results of this project may have rippling effects on the study of other estrildid finches and songbirds that exhibit this behavior, and coupled with other recent work with wild populations, may change what we thought we knew about the social dynamics of these birds and better inform wildlife management practices.
